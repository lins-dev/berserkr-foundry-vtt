# The Game

## Tests
Tests are made against a Difficulty Rating (DR). To make a test, roll **d20 ± your ability**. You succeed if the result is equal to or greater than the DR. Creatures don't use abilities, they just roll a d20 against DR.

Example: 'Disarm a Frost Giant's bear trap. Test Swift DR12.' or 'Resist the cursed poison of Nidhogg's fang. Test Fortitude DR14.'

| DR  | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| DR6 | So simple that people laugh at you if you fail. |
| DR8 | Routine, but some chance of failure. |
| DR10 | Pretty simple but not simple enough to not Test. |
| DR12 | Normal. |
| DR14 | Difficult. |
| DR16 | Really hard. |
| DR18 | Nigh impossible. |

Sometimes you will be granted advantage or disadvantage on a roll.
- **Advantage:** Roll twice and take the best result.
- **Disadvantage:** Roll twice and take the worst result.

## Carrying Capacity
You can carry Might⁺⁸ normal-sized items without a problem. After that, when testing Might and Swift, the DR increases by 2 (from 12 to 14, etc.). It is impossible to carry more than twice Might⁺⁸.

Normal-sized items include things like bones, heads, and axes, but not heavy objects like chests, beasts, or fallen enemies.

## Fates
Fates are blessings or curses woven into your life by the Norns. Whether it's divine intervention, the pull of fate, or the favour of a forgotten deity, these threads guide your destiny, for good or ill.

Every character not made with optional classes begins with d2 Fates. To regain spent Fates, take a long rest and roll the designated die for your class (or d2 if playing without classes).

Use Fates to:
- Deal maximum damage with one attack.
- Reroll a dice roll (yours or an ally's).
- Reduce damage dealt to you by d6.
- Negate a critical hit or fumble.
- Reduce the DR of one Test by -4.

## Hit Points
If starting without a class, begin with Fortitude⁺ d10HP. In the worst case 1HP, but never less.

- **Zero HP = Broken**
- **Negative HP = Dead**

### Broken
Roll d6.
1. Fall unconscious for d4 rounds, then awaken with d4HP.
2. Broken or severed limb. Gain the fever condition.
3. Most of your teeth were knocked out of your mouth. Decrease current Guile by 1.
4. Roll on the Debilitating Injuries Table.
5. Haemorrhage. Death in d2 hours unless treated. All Tests are DR16.
6. Dead.

### Debilitating Injuries
Roll d12.
1. Lost An Eye: Disadvantage on ranged attacks.
2. Frostbitten Fingers: Struggle with fine motor tasks.
3. Cracked Ribs: Easily fatigued after battle.
4. Venom-Scarred: Disadvantage on Tests for poison or fever.
5. Lame Leg: Disadvantage on Tests against being knocked prone.
6. Head Injury: Frequent headaches and impaired focus.
7. Frozen Lung: Shortness of breath with physical effort.
8. Crippled Hand: Reduced grip strength.
9. Deafened In One Ear: Difficulty pinpointing sounds.
10. Necrotic Burns: Half any HP recovered from resting.
11. Soul-Sapped: Disadvantage on Might Tests.
12. Cracked Spine: Stiff movements, vulnerable to re-injury. Next time you are broken, you DIE.

## Battle
Players roll for both their attacks and defence.
- Melee: Test Might DR12
- Ranged: Test Guile DR12
- Defence: Test Swift DR12
- Runes: Test Wits DR12
- Parry: Test Fortitude DR+3 (once per battle)

### Initiative
Players and GMs can use either Individual Initiative or Group Initiative.

**Group Initiative** (Recommended for fast paced combat):
At the top of every round, Roll d6.
- 1-3 Enemies go first.
- 4-6 PCs go first.

**Individual Initiative**:
Test Swift +d6 to create the initiative order—highest result goes first. In the event of a tie, roll again.

### How Long is a Round?
A round represents roughly six seconds in the game world. Unless otherwise stated, PCs may attack once per round.

A round is fleeting—a heartbeat in the cold, uncaring darkness of the world. It is only enough time for a strike of steel, a frantic movement, the casting of an ancient Rune, or a desperate grasp for survival.

Each round is a moment suspended in the shadow of fate. Only ten of these heartbeats make a minute, and in that time, a soul may witness unspeakable horrors or find themselves teetering on the edge of steel's cold grasp.

### Armour Degradation
When armour is damaged penalties to Might and Swift Tests are not modified.
Armour reduced below tier 1 is ruined and cannot be repaired (for armour repair costs, see the Equipment Table).

### Unarmed Attack
An unarmed attack's damage is d2.

## Parry
In the heat of battle, there's that split second where you have to choose—do you stand firm, or do you move with the attack, using its own force to strike back? Once per battle, you can choose to parry an incoming blow instead of just relying on defence.

To parry, Test Fortitude against the DR of the incoming melee attack +3. If you succeed, you don't just block the attack—you turn it back on your enemy, dealing your weapon's damage dice immediately. If you fail, the attack lands. It's a counterstrike born from the energy of the blow meant to harm you, where you become both defender and aggressor in one fluid motion, balancing on the knife-edge between survival and destruction.

And if fate smiles on you with a critical hit, your counter-attack lands with doubled ferocity, as if time itself froze just to give you this perfect moment of retribution.

| Critical Hit (Natural 20) | Effect |
|---------------------------|--------|
| Attack | Double damage, armour is also reduced 1 tier. |
| Defence | PC gets a free attack. |
| Parry | Double damage, armour is also reduced 1 tier. |

| Fumble (Natural 1) | Effect |
|--------------------|--------|
| Attack | The weapon breaks or is lost. |
| Defence | PC takes double damage and armour is reduced 1 tier. |
| Parry | The weapon breaks or is lost, PC takes double damage and armour is reduced 1 tier. |

## Conditions

### Poisoned
A deadly substance courses through your veins, sapping your strength and clarity.
Test Fortitude DR12 at the beginning of each round. On a failure, you suffer d4 damage and have disadvantage on all Tests for the round. Outside of battle, all Tests are made with disadvantage. The poison lasts until it is purged using an antidote, magic, or after d4 hours.

### Fever
Raging heat consumes your body, sapping your energy and making you delirious.
Test Fortitude DR10 at the start of each day. On a failure, you are fatigued until you take a long rest, and your Wits Tests are made with disadvantage. If the fever persists for more than 3 days it becomes life-threatening, and each subsequent day requires you to Test Fortitude DR14 to avoid losing d8HP.

### Blindness
Your sight is stripped away, leaving you vulnerable in a world of shadows and danger.
All attacks and Tests relying on vision are made with disadvantage. PCs have advantage on defence when attacked by a blinded enemy. You must Test Swift DR12 when the GM deems appropriate when travelling. If you fail, you take d2 damage as you stumble or injure yourself. Blindness may be temporary or permanent, depending on the cause.

### Fatigued
Exhaustion weighs down your limbs, dulling your reflexes and slowing your movements.
While fatigued, you suffer disadvantage on all Might and Swift Tests. Any physical exertion (running, fighting, etc.) requires you to Test Fortitude DR10. On a failure, you collapse, unable to act for 1 round. Fatigue lasts until you rest for at least 6 hours without interruption.

### Stunned
A stunned creature cannot move, parry or attack during the next round. Attacks against a stunned creature are made with advantage. Defence tests while stunned are made with disadvantage.

### Prone
While prone, defence and attacks are made with disadvantage. Attacks against prone enemies are made with advantage. Takes an action to stand up.

## Rest
In this unforgiving world, rest is essential, but even sleep may not be enough when the body is racked by hunger, sickness, or exhaustion.
- **Short Rest:** Take a brief respite. Roll d4 to restore a small amount of HP.
- **Long Rest:** A full night's sleep/six hours of uninterrupted rest restores d6HP.

If you go without both food and water during rest, you regain no HP.

### Starvation and Dehydration
After two days without food, you begin losing d4HP per day to starvation. After two days without water, you lose d6HP per day until you find drinkable water.

## Reaction
When you encounter creatures whose reaction is uncertain—be they gods, giants, or spirits—roll 2d6 to determine their mood, shaped by the whims of the Norns and ancient grudges.

| 2d6 | Reaction | Description |
|-----|----------|-------------|
| 2-3 | Doomed by Fate | You have offended the gods or stirred ancient grudges. The creature will attack immediately, seeing you as cursed or an enemy of fate. |
| 4-6 | Wrath of the Old Gods | Their blood burns hot with rage. A fight is near, but they may allow you a single word before they strike. |
| 7-8 | Indifferent as the Dead | They regard you with cold indifference, as one might a fly buzzing in the long dark of Fimbulwinter. You are beneath their notice... for now. |
| 9-10 | Guarded Respect | They acknowledge your presence with a wary nod. They may not be friendly, but they recognise strength when they see it. |
| 11-12 | Fate's Favour | The Norns smile upon you. The creature is unusually helpful, offering aid or information, perhaps sensing a shared destiny. |

## Improvement
The Norns weave the threads of every mortals fate, but it is the GM who decides when a character has earned the right to improve. Whether through surviving a deadly battle, accomplishing a feat of cunning, or making a sacrifice in blood and sweat, improvement is never granted lightly.

When the GM declares the time is right, do the following:

### Increase HP
Roll 6d10. If the result is equal to or greater than your current maximum HP, increase your maximum HP by d6.

### Ability Changes
Roll d6 for each ability.
If the result is equal to or greater than the current ability, increase that ability by 1, up to a maximum of +6. If the result is 1 the ability decreases by 1, but never below -3.

## Holmgang
A holmgang is a sacred duel, used to settle disputes and prove one's honour. Both participants agree to fight within a marked square, with the gods watching closely.
Breaking the rules or showing cowardice brings divine wrath.

### Setup
- The challenger and challenged agree on the terms (to the death, submission, or first blood).
- The duel takes place inside a marked square. Leaving the square results in immediate defeat and divine wrath.
- At the start of each round, both duellists roll d6 for initiative. The higher result goes first.

### Rules of Combat
- **Leaving the Square:** If a duellist leaves the square (voluntarily or pushed out), they immediately lose the duel and incur Moderate Wrath of the gods.
- **Victory Conditions:** The duel ends when one duellist is killed, surrenders, or first blood is drawn (depending on the terms). A clean surrender is respected, but failure to honour the terms of the duel could attract wrath.

### Divine Wrath
Leaving the square or acting dishonourably triggers Moderate Wrath of the gods. The GM rolls on the Moderate Wrath Table for either Odin or Loki.

### Outcomes
- **Victory:** The victor adds a tale to their Saga and possibly gains favour from the gods.
- **Defeat:** The loser must submit to the terms or face the consequences of dishonour.
- **Dishonour:** Leaving the square or breaking the rules triggers Moderate Wrath of the gods, punishing the dishonoured duellist.

## Flying
A Duel of Wit and Resilience. Flying is a verbal contest where two participants hurl insults to prove their wit and mental resilience. The winner is determined by their ability to both craft clever insults and endure the opponent's verbal attacks.

### Setup
The flying lasts for 3 rounds. The GM and other players adjust the DR based on the player's insult:
- DR-2: Good insult.
- DR12: Baseline.
- DR+2: Weak insult.

### Rules of Flying
In each round:
1. The opponent insults the PC.
2. The PC insults their opponent.
3. The insult is judged using the table above.
4. The player Tests Guile with appropriate DR.
5. Repeat.

To win the round, the player must succeed on the Guile Test. Failing means the opponent wins that round.

**Divine Influence (Optional):** The GM may roll d6 before the flying to determine divine involvement:
- 1-2 Divine Favour: The player gains +2 to one Test during the duel.
- 3-4 Neutral: No divine intervention.
- 5-6 Divine Wrath: The player takes -2 to one Test during the duel.

### Outcomes
- **Victory:** After 3 rounds, the participant who has won 2 or more rounds wins the flying. Gain +1 to all Guile Tests for the next d4 days. The PC gains advantage on all Tests related to the defeated opponent for d4 days.
- **Defeat:** The character suffers disadvantage on all social Tests for the next d4 days. If the player loses all rounds, their character may also incur Moderate Wrath.

## Navigating the Realms
Travel between the Nine Realms across Yggdrasil's branches is dangerous and unpredictable. The PCs must summon a gate and then navigate The World Tree to move between realms.

### Step I: Summoning a Gate
Player Characters must first summon a gate by performing a ritual which uses a totem. The totem must embody the essence of the desired realm. Its destruction during the ritual reflects the sacrifice required to breach the barriers between worlds.

**Totems by Realm:**
- **Midgard:** A thing of Earth. A stone worn smooth by a river, a handful of fertile soil or an oak branch tied with twine—simple, grounded, and tied to the mortal plane.
- **Asgard:** A thing of Glory. A fragment of a warrior's banner, a golden coin minted in triumph or a medal won in battle. Anything that carries the weight of victory and honour.
- **Vanaheim:** A thing of Nature. A sprig of wild mistletoe, a freshly bloomed flower, or a vial of untouched spring water. Whatever captures the raw, untamed beauty of nature.
- **Jotunheim:** A thing of Frost. A shard of ice that refuses to melt, a giant's toe or the frozen pelt of a beast that roamed the tundra.
- **Alfheim:** A thing of Light. A crystal that catches sunlight, a drop of honey from a flower-fed bee or a white feather. Anything radiant and ephemeral.
- **Svartalfheim:** A thing of Value. A gem cut from the deepest mines, a flawless ring of silver or a rare coin steeped in greed and pride.
- **Niflheim:** A thing of Shadows. A scrap of blackened cloth, a raven's feather or a handful of ash that refuses to drift away. Symbols of mystery and the unseen.
- **Muspelheim:** A thing of Flame. A charred piece of wood, a shard of obsidian born in fire or the embers of a dying flame caught in a glass jar.
- **Helheim:** A thing of Death. A bone from a fallen beast, a dried black rose or a shroud woven for the dead. Each carries the weight of life's inevitable end.

Test Guile DR12 to perform the ritual and summon a gate. Whether successful or not, the totem is destroyed during the ritual.
- **Critical Success:** The gate is constructed and you may pass straight through to the desired realm.
- **Success:** The gate is constructed and you may pass through onto the branching limbs of Yggdrasil.
- **Failure:** No gate is made, and you must try again later.

### Step II: Navigating Yggdrasil
Once on Yggdrasil, the party must navigate its branches to reach the desired realm. Test Wits DR12. On a success, they arrive at the intended realm. On a failure, a mishap occurs (roll on the Yggdrasil Table).

### Yggdrasil Table (d100)
(Roll d100 and consult the table on page 65 of the PDF for specific mishaps. The table includes various outcomes such as arriving in a different realm, being attacked by creatures, etc.)

### Nidhogg's Arrival
If a player fumbles when attempting to navigate The World Tree, they inadvertently call the attention of Nidhogg, the ancient dragon that gnaws at the roots of Yggdrasil. They must fight or flee.

### Quick Summary
- Test Guile DR12 with an appropriate totem to summon a gate.
  - Critical Success: Bypass Yggdrasil—the gate leads straight to the desired realm.
  - Success: Gate to Yggdrasil is summoned.
  - Failure: No gate summoned, try again later.
- Test Wits DR12 to navigate Yggdrasil.
  - Success: Travel to the desired realm.
  - Failure: A mishap (roll on the Yggdrasil Table).
  - Fumble: Nidhogg appears, forcing the players to either fight or flee.

## Favour & Wrath of the Gods
The GM invokes Favour or Wrath during key moments in the campaign:
- After completing a significant quest or task that impacts the balance of power between Odin and Loki.
- When characters improve or gain new abilities, drawing the attention of the gods.
- At major turning points in the story, such as surviving a harrowing battle or making a critical decision.
- Occasionally, when a character prays to a god or offers a sacrifice.
- After a holmgang.
- If a character spectacularly fails at flying.
- When attempting to use a Rune that results in a fumble.
- When the GM sees fit.

### Surprise Favour or Wrath
In most situations, players won't know whether they'll receive Favour or Wrath—this is left to the GM's discretion or decided by a coin flip. The GM flips a coin secretly to determine the outcome:
- Heads: The character receives Favour.
- Tails: The character receives Wrath.

### Tracker Influence
The Ragnarok tracker determines which god's Favour or Wrath the characters are subject to:
- Leaning Toward Loki: The characters might receive Loki's Favour but risk Odin's Wrath.
- Leaning Toward Odin: The characters might receive Odin's Favour but risk Loki's Wrath.
- Neutral Positions: The GM has more freedom to decide which god responds, depending on the characters' actions.

### Rolling on the Tables:
Once Favour or Wrath is decided, the player rolls d12 and the GM consults the appropriate table:
- Minor: Balanced situations or minor moments of Favour or Wrath.
- Moderate: The tracker is moderately aligned with one god.
- Severe: The tracker is heavily aligned with one god, or after significant story events.

### When the Effect Happens:
The effect generally won't happen immediately—it takes place at the next appropriate moment:
- In Battle: It might manifest as sudden divine intervention or a catastrophe.
- During Travel: Divine favour could smooth the journey, while wrath might create new dangers.
- In Social Encounters: The gods could subtly shift the balance of negotiations or interactions.

The GM introduces these effects when they will have the greatest narrative impact, ensuring the gods' presence feels powerful and influential in the story.

### Examples
**Example 1: Quest Completion (Tracker closer to Loki)**
1. After completing a quest that aligns with Loki's interests, the GM decides it's time to invoke the gods.
2. The GM flips a coin secretly to see if the party receives Favour or Wrath. The coin lands on heads, meaning Loki's Favour.
3. The GM asks a player to roll d12 and then consults the Loki Moderate Favour table, resulting in Jörmungandr's Blessing, which is triggered the next time they attack, dealing an additional d4 poison damage.

**Example 2: Characters Improve (Tracker closer to Odin)**
1. After the party Improves, the GM decides that the gods have taken notice.
2. The GM flips a coin to see whether Odin's Favour or Loki's Wrath will follow. The flip lands on tails—Loki's Minor Wrath.
3. The player rolls d12 and the GM checks the Loki Minor Wrath table and gets Nidhogg's Hunger; next time the character rests or eats, they'll realise they've lost d4 food and water.

**Example 3: Unexpected divine response (Tracker balanced)**
1. After surviving a difficult battle, the GM invokes divine attention. The tracker is balanced, so the GM decides to flip the coin publicly.
2. The coin lands on heads, signalling Favour. The GM decides the character will receive Odin's Favour, and the player rolls d12 on the Odin Minor Favour table, gaining Odin's Wisdom, which grants them insight for their next challenge.
